## Abstract: 

Growing a dev team while preserving your culture and mentoring junior devs sounds difficult, maybe impossible. Especially in this era of AI, how do you successfully build a home-grown team of young talent? I'll explore the success we've had with exactly that at Atomic Object, and practices you can implement today in your own companies to do the same.
## Community talk notes:

Atomic Object invests heavily in early-career devs — roughly 300 hours of
mentorship covering both technical and consulting skills, plus book groups,
feedback practice, demos, and tech talks. What's different isn't the hires, it's
the investment.

### Reframing the image of juniors
The common (wrong) view is that juniors are:
- **cheap** — there to free up seniors,
- **replaceable** — no institutional knowledge,
- **rentals** — can't build new technical capacity.

Reframe them as:
- an **investment**, not cheap labor,
- **not replaceable** — they're diverse and unique, which matters more as work
  shifts,
- **invaluable**, not rentals.

### Why this matters in the AI era
With AI, human effort is moving into more complex and chaotic modes of working.
The trap: AI writes more code → the dev looks more "productive" → free them for
higher-level work → more output → repeat. That loop can become a death spiral if
you stop growing people.

The skills have changed, but the need for people has not.

### What works
Heavily invest in early-career mentorship, hire for fit and the future, and do
more interesting work. Atomic sees ~2 years of training pay off in ~5-year
retention.
